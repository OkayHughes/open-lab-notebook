Linear algebra

Linear structure

Why would we want more generality than this?

"Linearity" typically looks like various extensions of the 1D linear system y=mx+by=mx+by=mx+b. We want to be able to multiply by coefficients (e.g., mmm) and add potential solutions (x,bx, bx,b) together. You can verify using the laws of matrix multiplication that A(x+y)=Ax+AyA(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}) = A\mathbf{x} + A\mathbf{y}A(x+y)=Ax+Ay, and that A(ax+y)=aAx+AyA(a\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}) = aA\mathbf{x} + A\mathbf{y} A(ax+y)=aAx+Ay (a∈Ra \in \mathbb{R}a∈R). These equation show that vector addition and scalar multiplication, even if you've only encountered them as "joining arrows" or "scaling arrows" in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, are "preserved", in some sense, by matrix multiplication.

In a sense that can be made extremely mathematically precise, the operations of differentiation and integration can be used to formulate linear equations (but which are MUCH harder to solve/characterize than systems in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, because they are infinite dimensional). However, in their most general form, there isn't a very natural way to write differentiation and integration as a matrix (integration can sometimes be thought of as an infinite-dimensional matrix, in a limiting sense, but differentiation in infinite dimensions absolutely cannot be thought of this way). This leads us to the question "what properties do matrices and more general linear operations share". This is one reason to define "linear operators", of which matrices, integration, and differentiation are examples. In finite dimensions, operators always have a matrix representation.

Inner product structure, duals

An inner product is an additional operation ⟨⋅,⋅⟩\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle ⟨⋅,⋅⟩ that one can add to a vector space. It must satisfy certain requirements. It is a generalization of the dot product on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, ⟨x,y⟩=∑kxkyk \langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle = \sum_k x_k y_k ⟨x,y⟩=∑k​xk​yk​. The aforementioned requirements are the minimal set of constraints required to guarantee that the inner product you defined on your vector space VVV, gets you the properties that make the dot product useful.

Why are orthogonal bases good?

First we need to talk about what bases are. In Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, a basis is a set of nnn vectors bk\mathbf{b}_kbk​ such that any x∈Rn \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^nx∈Rn can be written as ∑kbkbk=x \sum_k b_k \mathbf{b}_k = \mathbf{x}∑k​bk​bk​=x in exactly one way.
